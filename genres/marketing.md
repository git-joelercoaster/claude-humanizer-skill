# Genre Profile: Marketing & Sales Copy

SaaS landing pages. Product descriptions. Sales emails. Ad copy. Email campaigns. Social ads. Pitch decks.

## The Goal

**Authentic enthusiasm backed by specifics. Tell the truth about benefits. Show, don't sell.**

Marketing is the most AI-dense writing (30+ tells per piece). The paradox: the more you try to sell, the more obvious the AI becomes.

## What Changes

### DO Strip

- All urgency language (fake scarcity, countdown, "limited time")
- Buzzwords (revolutionary, game-changing, cutting-edge, paradigm, synergy, empower, unlock)
- Fake FOMO (thousands already, don't miss out)
- Three-part transformation narrative (before-problem-after)
- Unsubstantiated claims
- Exclamation point abuse
- Manufactured drama
- Generic upbeat closer
- Features presented as benefits

### DO NOT Strip

- Enthusiasm (if genuine)
- Benefits (what the user gets)
- Specific data (numbers, metrics, proof)
- Personality (if it fits the brand)
- Honesty about trade-offs
- Simple language (accessibility)
- Calls to action (they're needed)

## Signs of AI in Marketing Copy

**Problem areas:**
- "Revolutionary AI-powered platform that transforms how businesses unlock value"
- "Join thousands of forward-thinking companies experiencing unprecedented growth"
- "Limited spots available — sign up today before it's too late"
- "Game-changing solution that will disrupt your industry"
- "Seamlessly integrate cutting-edge capabilities into your workflow"
- Every benefit phrased as transformation
- Multiple exclamation points
- FOMO language on every page
- Three-step narrative (we solved, you win, future is bright)

**Good marketing:**
- Says what it does in first sentence
- Backs claims with numbers
- Shows real customers (honest quotes, including caveats)
- Acknowledges limitations
- Specific about who it's for
- Simple language
- One clear call to action

## The Marketing Trap

Humans don't buy because of buzzwords. They buy because:
1. The product actually solves their problem
2. They trust you're not lying
3. The price is worth the solution

AI tries to sell through language. Humans sell through credibility.

Specificity = credibility. Vagueness = AI.

## Common Marketing AI Mistakes

**Mistake 1: Buzzword overload**
> "Leverage our cutting-edge platform to seamlessly integrate transformative capabilities that unlock unprecedented value and empower your team to drive exponential growth through innovative solutions."

**Fix:**
> "Cut your data processing time from hours to minutes."

---

**Mistake 2: Three-part transformation narrative**
> "Before: You're manually analyzing data. Problem: It's taking 20 hours a week. After: RevisionAI does it in 1 hour."

This is fine *once*. But every section follows this beat.

**Fix:**
> Vary the structure. Sometimes just show the benefit. Sometimes address an objection. Sometimes tell a story.

---

**Mistake 3: Fake FOMO**
> "Join 5,000+ companies already using RevisionAI. Limited spots available."

(There are no limited spots. You're SaaS. It scales.)

**Fix:**
> "5,000+ companies use RevisionAI" (if true)
> or skip it entirely if it's not differentiating

---

**Mistake 4: Unsubstantiated claims**
> "Revolutionary AI engine powered by state-of-the-art machine learning algorithms."

What does this mean? What's it actually do?

**Fix:**
> "Uses GPT-4 to analyze your data and spot patterns in seconds."

---

**Mistake 5: Fake enthusiasm**
> "It's AMAZING! You'll be THRILLED! This will TRANSFORM your life!!!"

Fix:** Genuine enthusiasm about real benefits:
> "Most people are shocked how fast this is. One customer cut their report time from 2 days to 1 hour."

---

**Mistake 6: Features instead of benefits**
> "AI-powered machine learning engine with real-time data processing capabilities and advanced analytics features."

**Fix:**
> "See what's actually happening in your data right now, not yesterday's report."

---

## Checkpoints Before Launch

- [ ] Can I explain the product in one sentence? (If not, it's too vague)
- [ ] Did I include at least one specific number?
- [ ] Are benefits stated clearly (not "cutting-edge," but "cuts time by 60%")?
- [ ] Do real testimonials include a caveat or minor criticism? (More credible)
- [ ] Did I avoid: revolutionary, game-changing, transformative, cutting-edge, empower, unlock, paradigm, seamless?
- [ ] Is there anything I'm claiming that I can't prove?
- [ ] Would a skeptical friend believe this?
- [ ] Is there a clear call to action?
- [ ] Did I acknowledge who this *isn't* for?

## Voice Calibration for Marketing Teams

Look for:
- **Tone** (formal? Casual? Between?)
- **Emoji usage** (if any, where and why?)
- **Enthusiasm level** (reserved? Energetic? How?)
- **Feature vs benefit ratio** (how much do they explain features?)
- **Specificity** (exact numbers? Ranges? Approximate?)
- **Testimonial style** (what do good reviews sound like?)
- **CTA style** (how direct? How many?)

Match in rewrites.

## Marketing by Channel

**Landing Page:**
- Most important: benefit in headline
- Specificity: numbers, not claims
- Social proof: real testimonials with minor caveats
- CTA: clear and single

**Email Campaign:**
- Start with why you're emailing (they did something)
- Specific benefit (not vague)
- Clear next step
- Personal tone (sounds like one person wrote it)

**Ads (social/search):**
- Headline: specific benefit
- Copy: explain one thing
- CTA: simple verb (Sign up, Learn more, See demo)

**Sales Page:**
- What is this? (first line)
- Who is it for?
- How much does it cost?
- What do customers say?

**Outreach Email:**
- Why you're reaching out (specific, not generic)
- Quick value prop (one sentence)
- Ask (clear)
- CTA (link or question)

---

## The Marketing Virtue

Great marketing tells the truth. It's confident because the product is good.

AI marketing is inflated because the product isn't clear enough to stand on its own.

Specificity is the highest form of persuasion.

Truth sells better than hype.
